## 1. Industries being hollowed out by AI — who is losing income, with evidence

The single most important fact for the May 2026 landscape: white-collar employment has cracked, and the cracks are quantifiable. In Q1 2026 alone, the tech industry laid off 78,557 workers — 47.9% of those cuts (37,638 positions) were explicitly attributed to AI and workflow automation ([Source 1](https://www.tomshardware.com/tech-industry/tech-industry-lays-off-nearly-80-000-employees-in-the-first-quarter-of-2026-almost-50-percent-of-affected-positions-cut-due-to-ai)). Oracle alone cut 10,000+ positions across divisions; Cloudflare cut 1,100 (20% of its workforce). Block (Square / Cash App) compressed from ~10,000 to under 6,000 employees in early March 2026, the largest single workforce reduction explicitly attributed to AI in corporate history.

What's hollowing isn't an industry — it's a **role-shape**: digitisable, repetitive cognitive work executed at the entry-to-mid level. Job-posting data (H1 2024 → H2 2025) shows the specific roles in free-fall: Customer Service Representatives down 24.9%, Customer Support down 28.8%, Copywriters down 28.1%, Data Entry down 34.1%, Telemarketers down 29.3% ([Source 2](https://www.jobspikr.com/report/ai-layoffs-2026-roi-reality-check/)). These are the jobs that built the modern middle class — administrative assistants ($40–55K), junior analysts ($55–75K), marketing coordinators ($50–70K), paralegals ($50–65K). All structurally exposed; postings for new roles in these categories are not coming back.

Legal is the canonical case. Priestley's headline thesis — "the minute an AI learns how to be a lawyer in one place, it can be a lawyer in every place" — has receipts: Baker McKenzie cut between 600 and 1,000 employees (up to 10% of its global workforce) in 2026 in an explicit shift toward AI ([Source 3](https://www.shortform.com/podcast/episode/the-diary-of-a-ceo-with-steven-bartlett-2026-03-16-episode-summary-daniel-priestley-plumbers-will-earn-more-than-lawyers-i-predicted-2008-now-i-m-warning-about-2029)). Roughly 31% of tasks for new legal associates and paralegals (document review, legal research) are now handled by specialised AI tools. Fortune corroborates the structural diagnosis: entry-level tech hiring has fallen 50% since 2019, and over 30% of US workers could see at least 50% of their tasks disrupted by generative AI ([Source 4](https://fortune.com/2026/03/19/plumbers-outearning-lawyers-daniel-priestley-blue-collar-vs-white-collar-american-dream/)).

Two important counter-signals that anyone narrating "AI is taking all the jobs" should hold in mind. **First, there's AI-washing.** Cognizant's Chief AI Officer (quoted in Source 1): *"Sometimes AI becomes the scapegoat from a financial perspective, like when a company hired too many, or they want to resize."* Forrester predicts 50% of AI-attributed layoffs will result in "quiet rehire" — often offshore, often at lower salaries. **Second, the ROI evidence is grim.** 95% of enterprise AI pilot programmes failed to deliver measurable financial returns; only 6% of organisations qualify as genuine AI high performers ([Source 2](https://www.jobspikr.com/report/ai-layoffs-2026-roi-reality-check/)). The cuts are happening; whether they stick is a different question (which Section 3 returns to via the Klarna case).

**Implication for a £0 operator:** the hollowing is *real* but the surface area being hollowed is narrow and specific. Repetitive cognitive output (generic copy, basic legal research, tier-1 customer service, basic data entry) is dead. Anything requiring judgment, embodiment, relationship, or proprietary context is not. The opportunity isn't "compete with AI" — it's "sell to the people getting displaced" (Section 2) or "do the work AI provably can't" (Section 3).

## 2. Industries where AI creates new buyer demand — who suddenly needs help and will pay

Three distinct buyer pools have meaningfully expanded in 2024–2026. Each is large, cash-bearing, and structurally under-served by the existing solo-operator playbook.

**Pool A — UK trades and field-service businesses.** This is Priestley's "plumbers earn more than lawyers" thesis reduced to operations. The UK needs around 1.3 million new tradespeople and 350,000 apprentices by 2033, with an immediate shortfall of 250,000+ workers needed for housing and infrastructure targets ([Source 5](https://www.logic4training.co.uk/insights/are-electricians-in-demand-uk/)). Plumbers and heating engineers face a 59,000 shortfall; electricians have 9,365 unfilled vacancies. UK electricians earn £39K average (self-employed specialists £55K+); self-employed electrician business owners average ~£65K. The demand drivers are durable: EV charging infrastructure, heat-pump rollout, grid upgrades, net-zero electrification — all of which require physical-skill labour through 2030+.

The B2B implication is the under-developed part: these are cash-flush small businesses operating on paper. ServiceTitan's own marketing copy describes the current state as *"customer info scribbled on paper, typed into Excel, or scattered across multiple systems"* ([Source 6](https://www.servicetitan.com/blog/plumbing-crm)). The incumbents (ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, Commusoft) are priced and architected for 20–500 employee operations. For a 1–5 person crew, the entire category is *over-served by complexity and under-served by usability*. Crews are buying — they're buying badly. A small operator with a £29–49/month tool aimed at the bottom segment is not competing with the incumbents; it's competing with the spreadsheet.

**Pool B — AI-augmented freelancers and consultants.** Upwork's 2026 in-demand-skills report shows AI-related freelance skills grew 109% year-over-year, vs. 23% for other top skills — and the AI-enabled freelancer earns ~40% more per hour than the equivalent unaided peer ([Source 7](https://www.selfemployed.com/news/ai-freelance-skills-demand-2026/)). Specific segments: AI video generation/editing demand grew 329% YoY, AI integration 178%, AI data annotation 154%, AI chatbot dev 71%. Practitioners who integrate AI workflows report deliverables that took 6 hours now take 2.5 — at the same rate, tripling hourly profit. There are now operators with £150/hour earning capacity who didn't exist as a category 18 months ago, and **they have unmet needs**: positioning, productisation, distribution, and the literal skill-acquisition itself.

The consulting market mirrors this. 78% of UK consultants name "digital technology and AI" as their primary growth driver; the consulting market is bifurcating, with boutique/independent firms expanding while mid-market firms shrink ([Source 8](https://www.alpha-sense.com/resources/research-articles/consulting-industry-trends/)). About 77% of business leaders report AI is increasing demand for *specialised, fractional talent*. Translation: there are now solo and 2-3 person advisory practices doing work that big-4 firms used to monopolise — and they need everything a real business needs (CRM, contracts, content, lead-gen, packaging), but designed for a one-person operation, not a 50-person agency.

**Pool C — White-collar workers in active reinvention.** This is the pool the playbook didn't fully name. The displaced, the anxious-but-employed, and the early-career professionals who can read the trajectory are *actively buying* services that help them reposition. This is the audience behind every "how I left law for X" newsletter and every reskilling-into-AI course. They have salaries (for now), savings, and a heightened sense of urgency. They don't need cheaper info; they need *trust*, *specificity to their case*, and *embodied presence from someone who's actually done the move*. Generic "career reinvention" content is saturated; specific to a vertical (e.g., "lawyer → AI product manager") and an operator who's done the path is not.

**Implication for a £0 operator:** all three pools want services *built by a recognisable human who has been in their shoes*. Pool A buyers want trade-specific software made by someone who's worked in trades or sat alongside them. Pool B buyers want positioning advice from someone who has productised. Pool C buyers want reinvention guidance from someone who has reinvented. Operator authenticity isn't optional in these three pools — it's the moat.

## 3. Skills and services humans still prefer from humans

Three categories of work pass the human-preferred test convincingly in May 2026: **embodied/relational care, trust-anchored advice, and judgement-under-novelty**.

**Embodied/relational care** is the cleanest case. The therapeutic-relationship literature is robust: clinical effectiveness depends on the felt experience of being seen, on a clinician's ability to track a client's nervous system in real time, on embodied presence during shame and grief — none of which AI can replicate ([Source 9](https://therapywisdom.com/will-ai-replace-therapists/)). Clients want three things AI inadequately provides: connection (not just information), safety (felt experience, not algorithmic reassurance), and being seen (through genuine presence). The same applies — at lower stakes but the same shape — to coaching, mentoring, somatic / bodywork practitioners, and any service where the *relationship is the product*. AI is augmenting the periphery of these (note-taking, between-session prompts, scheduling) but cannot replace the central act.

**Trust-anchored advice** is the second category. The Klarna case is the cleanest 2025–2026 counter-example to "AI eats customer service": Klarna announced in 2024 that an AI customer service system had replaced 700 agents — by 2025 they were rehiring agents ([Source 2](https://www.jobspikr.com/report/ai-layoffs-2026-roi-reality-check/)). When customers are anxious, confused, or making a high-stakes decision (financial, legal, medical, professional), they prefer human escalation even if the AI could technically resolve their issue. This generalises: financial planning for major life events, legal advice on consequential cases, medical/diagnostic conversation, real-estate transactions, hiring decisions — all categories where a human's *responsibility-bearing* presence is the deliverable, not the information itself.

**Judgement-under-novelty** is the least obvious and the most under-priced. Generative AI is fundamentally interpolative — it's exceptional at restating, recombining, and accelerating known patterns, and unreliable at situations the training data hasn't seen well. Anything genuinely *new* (novel regulatory situation, a client whose specifics don't match any precedent, a strategic question whose answer requires reading a person not a database) still rewards a human practitioner with reps. This is *especially* true in regulated or relational verticals where being wrong has consequences the practitioner has to absorb personally.

There are two further categories often listed but worth being honest about:

- **Local/hands-on services** (trades, hair, beauty, fitness, childcare, hospitality) are human-preferred for the obvious physical reason, but they're not the £0-operator opportunity for *me as the practitioner* — they require either a local presence I can't deliver as a digital operator or a capital outlay vetoed by the brief. They are, however, the *buyers* (Section 2, Pool A).
- **Creative output with taste** (writing, design, video direction) is contested. AI does the production; humans still make most of the directional decisions. The honest read: this is human-preferred *for now*, but the boundary moves quarterly. Build into it cautiously and with a vertical anchor.

**The cross-cutting principle**: human-preferred services share an irreducible component — a human bearing responsibility for the outcome, present and identifiable. The corollary for a £0 operator is uncomfortable but useful: any business I build in 2026 should leverage me as the operator-product, not hide me behind a brand. The "anonymous SaaS company" template loses to "Finley does X, here's how" precisely because authenticity is the moat the frontier models can't compete on.

## 4. Underserved micro-niches with active spending

This is the section that gets specific. Below are the concrete niches with documented, in-2026 buyer evidence — not vibes, not training-data guesses. Each entry follows: *who's complaining, where, about what, current alternatives, plausible operator wedge.*

**Solo and 1–5 person trades operations.** Source [10](https://superframeworks.com/articles/untapped-underserved-micro-saas-niches) explicitly identifies the gap: ServiceTitan and Housecall Pro charge $200–500/month and are built for 20+ employee crews. 1–5 person crews are stuck on paper invoices, generic spreadsheets, and phone-based scheduling. UK plumbers and electricians ([Source 5](https://www.logic4training.co.uk/insights/are-electricians-in-demand-uk/)) sit squarely in this gap. **Operator wedge:** a mobile-first £29–49/month tool combining estimates + invoicing + scheduling + photo-doc, with positioning explicitly *not* aimed at the enterprise tier.

**Small landlords (sub-10 unit portfolios).** Repeated complaints on r/Landlord (per [Source 11](https://medium.com/@e2larsen/50-saas-ideas-pulled-straight-from-reddit-pain-points-a64569371691)): forgotten maintenance schedules, missing pest-control reminders, scattered lease docs, no system for verifying tenant income. Current alternatives are enterprise property management (overkill) or spreadsheets. **Operator wedge:** £10–20/month tool for rent reminders, maintenance scheduling, digital lease storage; optional Plaid-based income verification as a £5–10 per-screening upsell.

**Solo legal practitioners.** Recurring r/LawFirm complaints: client intake still by phone/mail; document drafting absent good-enough tools; reliance on enterprise software priced above their budget ([Source 11](https://medium.com/@e2larsen/50-saas-ideas-pulled-straight-from-reddit-pain-points-a64569371691)). The vertical-AI thesis ([Source 12](https://www.aimagicx.com/blog/vertical-ai-micro-saas-business-model-2026)) names "specialty pharmacy prior auth" at $2,000–5,000/month per customer — solo legal sits in the same shape. **Operator wedge:** an AI-paralegal that drafts documents and intake forms specifically for solo and 2-3 lawyer practices in a *named jurisdiction* (e.g., England & Wales), £49–99/month. Defensibility = jurisdiction-specific templates + workflow + (eventually) usage data the generic models can't see.

**Specialty food producers.** Producers turning over <£1M/year manually generate nutrition labels, track ingredient lots in spreadsheets, and handle wholesale orders by email ([Source 10](https://superframeworks.com/articles/untapped-underserved-micro-saas-niches)). Enterprise food ERPs cost £10K+/year. **Operator wedge:** £39–79/month compliance platform with label generation, lot tracking, wholesale orders. UK-specific compliance (FSA labelling rules, FIR 2014) is the moat.

**Wedding/event vendor coordination.** Vendors coordinate large events through 47-person group texts and endless email chains ([Source 10](https://superframeworks.com/articles/untapped-underserved-micro-saas-niches)). The Knot/Zola serve *couples*, not the 12–15 vendors who need shared logistics. **Operator wedge:** vendor-side platform with shared timelines, messaging, day-of coordination, £25–49/month.

**Tattoo/body-art studios.** Artists run via Instagram DMs, deposits via Venmo, paper consent forms. No mainstream SaaS addresses this niche specifically; generic salon software (Vagaro, Fresha) is built for hair ([Source 10](https://superframeworks.com/articles/untapped-underserved-micro-saas-niches)). **Operator wedge:** £29–59/month studio platform with booking, deposit collection, digital consent forms, waitlist management.

**Music teachers and solo instructors.** Per Source 10: teachers use Calendly + Venmo + Google Docs + texts. Existing platforms (My Music Staff, Fons) are dated and desktop-only. **Operator wedge:** £19–49/month modern-mobile platform with scheduling, billing, progress tracking, parent portals.

**Mobile auto detailers.** Booking via Instagram DMs, routing via Google Maps, payments via Cash App ([Source 10](https://superframeworks.com/articles/untapped-underserved-micro-saas-niches)). No incumbent serves the *traveling-between-locations* workflow. **Operator wedge:** £29–49/month mobile-first app with route optimisation, before/after photos, integrated payments.

**Small co-working operators (10–50 desks).** Stuck on spreadsheets and manual invoicing; can't justify £1,000+ smart-lock platforms ([Source 10](https://superframeworks.com/articles/untapped-underserved-micro-saas-niches)). **Operator wedge:** £49–99/month lightweight tool with member billing, hot-desk booking, basic access control.

**Newer 2025–2026 indie hacker patterns** ([Source 13](https://www.somethingsblog.com/2026/01/24/real-indie-hacker-success-stories-that-prove-its-still-possible-in-2026/)) corroborate the shape: Braden Dennis built an AI-native financial data-as-a-service tool to $500K+ MRR; Romàn Czerny did $27K MRR by validating via service then productising. The pattern is consistent — **narrow vertical + service-first validation + productisation**, not "AI tool for generic productivity".

**Cross-niche pattern (worth naming):** Source 11's takeaway holds — *"The most common frustration across industries is workflow. People aren't asking for more features. They're begging for fewer."* The operator wedge across every entry above is the same shape: take a category dominated by an enterprise incumbent priced for the wrong tier, and ship a mobile-first, opinionated, narrow-scope alternative at a fraction of the price for the under-served bottom of the market.

The list above is also a confidence check: nine niches with explicit Reddit-or-equivalent buyer complaints and explicit gap-vs-incumbent diagnoses. The pick in `decision.md` will narrow further based on operator-fit (`me-profile.md`), but the shape of the play is constrained.

## 5. Tombstone exercise — which niches get killed by next-gen AI in 12 months, which survive

Tombstone test (from the playbook): for each candidate niche, write the most likely cause-of-death epitaph for May 2027. If the most likely epitaph is "killed by next OpenAI release" or "absorbed into a frontier-model product", kill the candidate now. If the most likely epitaph is "operator burned out", "wedge customer never existed in numbers", or "we lost on distribution to a better operator" — the niche is *still in play*; the failure mode is operator-side, not structural.

Top five candidates from Sections 2 and 4, tombstoned:

**Niche 1: Solo and 1–5 person trades back-office software (UK).**
- *Most likely 2027 epitaph:* "Acquired or out-competed by a better-funded specialist (Powered Now, Tradify, Commusoft Lite) that figured out the bottom tier."
- *AI-killed?* No. The customer is a UK plumber/electrician using a paper diary; the buying decision is about ergonomics, mobile UX, and trust with a real human operator behind the product. A frontier-model release does not change this. The data is local, the workflow is regulated-ish, and the buyer prefers a person they can call. **Survives. In play.**

**Niche 2: Reskilling/coaching service for displaced or anxious white-collar workers (vertical-specific).**
- *Most likely 2027 epitaph:* "Saturated by 'AI-augmented professional' grifters; couldn't differentiate from low-cost cohort courses."
- *AI-killed?* Partially — generic career advice content is being commodity-shipped by AI, and there are now thousands of AI-tutor products. But the human-preferred categorisation (Section 3) protects the *trust-anchored advice* layer. A specific niche (e.g., "displaced UK paralegals → AI-augmented compliance contractors") with a real operator who's done that path is not at structural risk in 12 months. **Survives, conditional on operator-led delivery, not anonymous content.**

**Niche 3: Compliance + admin for UK specialty food producers (<£1M turnover).**
- *Most likely 2027 epitaph:* "Government changed the labelling regime mid-build and the existing schema became wrong."
- *AI-killed?* No. The moat is jurisdiction-specific compliance (FSA, FIR 2014, allergen rules), tied to identifiable regulatory text and update cadences. A frontier model can read regulations but cannot reliably *operate* a compliant workflow without the curated, version-controlled compliance logic the operator builds. The real risk is regulatory churn, not model release. **Survives.**

**Niche 4: Solo legal practitioner tooling for England & Wales.**
- *Most likely 2027 epitaph:* "OpenAI launched a 'small-law-firm' product and ate the generic intake / drafting layer."
- *AI-killed?* **Partially at risk.** If the wedge is "generic AI drafting for any UK lawyer" — yes, this dies. If the wedge is *jurisdiction-specific + workflow-deep + Solicitors-Regulation-Authority-aware* + supports proprietary firm data the model can't see, it survives. The decision rule: build only if the operator can credibly build the *workflow + compliance* layer, not the *AI generation* layer. **Survives only as a vertical-AI play, not as a wrapper.**

**Niche 5: Wedding / event vendor coordination platform.**
- *Most likely 2027 epitaph:* "Vendors went back to WhatsApp because we couldn't get all 12 of them onto the same tool."
- *AI-killed?* No — embodied/relational coordination, multi-party, low-stakes-per-event but high-emotional-stakes. A frontier model release does not change vendor coordination dynamics. The real risk is the classic chicken-and-egg / network-effect problem: hard to seed a side-of-market platform where every venue has to sign on. **Survives (the structural risk is distribution, not AI).**

Three further niches that *failed* the tombstone test in earlier consideration (kept here for honesty):

- **"AI prompt pack on Gumroad"** (used as the worked example in the playbook): killed by OpenAI absorbing native prompt-library/skills features. Dead.
- **"Generic AI productivity newsletter for knowledge workers"**: killed by oversaturation + AI Overviews suppressing top-of-funnel SEO. Dead as a *primary* play (still valid as a side-publishing channel).
- **"AI-powered logo / graphic design for small businesses"**: killed by Canva and frontier-model native generation. Dead.

**Pattern across surviving niches:** the moat lives in one (or more) of three places: (a) **regulated/jurisdiction-specific knowledge** that updates slowly and rewards the operator who tracks it; (b) **embodied/relational service** where a human in the loop is the product, not the cost; (c) **a workflow deep enough that the model can answer one prompt but cannot run the operation**. A candidate that has none of these — that lives only on "we have a slightly nicer prompt" — does not survive 12 months.

This is the screen `decision.md` will run on the final shortlist.

## What this lands the project on (ahead of `me-profile.md`)

The next document is the one-shot questionnaire. After it, `decision.md` will pick **one** business. Given the landscape:

- The pick will almost certainly be in either **Pool A** (B2B for UK trades / 1–5 person field-service crews) or **Pool C** (reskilling/coaching for displaced or transitioning white-collar workers), with **Pool B** (services-to-AI-augmented-freelancers) as the second-best fallback. Pool A and Pool C are where the £0 operator + evenings constraint + human-as-moat conditions stack up best.
- The pick will be **operator-led** (face/name/specifics) not anonymous-brand, because Section 3's evidence is uncompromising on that point in 2026.
- The pick will be **vertical-deep** (named regulator, named customer type, named region) not horizontal, because every surviving niche in Section 5 has that shape.
- The pick will sequence as **service → productise → optionally-software**, not the reverse, because every 2025–2026 indie-hacker success story I fetched (Habit Pixel, Czerny, Welsh, Koe) follows that order — and the rare exceptions had pre-existing audiences.

The questionnaire is what tells me *which* of these to go in on — the answer depends almost entirely on the operator's actual life, contacts, and skin in the game. That's the next file.
