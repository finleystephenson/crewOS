## Post 2 — Schedule for: +5 days (next Wednesday), ~12:30 UK

The constraints I set on Crew OS, written down so they're public:

— £0 budget. One allowed spend was the domain.
— No warm intros. No friends-of-friends. Cold acquisition only.
— No paid ads. Free tier everything.
— No "fake it till you make it" — every pilot gets a working tool, not a waiting list.

Three things this forces, that I'd otherwise dodge:

1. The offer has to do real work in v0. Nobody pilots vapourware.
2. Distribution has to be in places UK trades already are — Facebook groups, Reddit, the inbox — not where it's comfortable for me to post.
3. The pricing has to be defensible from day one. £49/month is the price because anything higher needs trust I haven't earned yet and anything lower can't sustain the work.

## Post 3 — Schedule for: +13 days, ~10:30 UK

Day 13 of Crew OS.

The voice-note-to-quote pipeline works end-to-end on the desk.

A tradesperson talks for two minutes about a job — "boiler swap, three-bed semi in Penrith, customer wants it before half-term, parts I'll need" — sends three photos of the existing setup, hits submit. Eight minutes later, a branded PDF quote in their pricing voice hits the customer's inbox, plus a four-touch follow-up sequence scheduled.

What it gets right out of the box:
— Job description tight enough that the customer doesn't need to ask "what does it include?"
— UK-specific terms: CIS-aware, VAT-aware where the trader is registered, plain English.

What it gets wrong without context:
— Prices. Until the trader spends ten minutes telling Crew OS their actual hourly rate and markups, the quote uses sensible defaults that need overriding.

The onboarding step that teaches Crew OS your pricing is the bit that turns a generic tool into your tool. That's the real product.

Pilots open this week. Day 14 tomorrow.

## Post 4 — Schedule for: +20 days, ~14:00 UK

Three things UK tradespeople have told me in pilot calls that I didn't expect:

1. The quote isn't the work they hate. The *follow-up* is. They write a quote in 40 minutes, send it, then forget it. The customer goes quiet. Three weeks later they remember and feel awkward chasing. They lose the job to whoever rang the customer back on day 4.

2. They don't want help with the trade-specific parts. They know their trade. They want help with the parts that aren't their trade — paperwork, VAT, CIS deductions, GDPR, customer chasing, invoicing. The bits a generic ServiceTitan does, plus the bits ServiceTitan doesn't bother with at this scale.

3. They'll trust a tool faster if it's *visibly built for them*. Generic CRM language ("pipelines", "leads", "deals") puts them off. "Quote", "job", "callback" lands.

Most of what I thought was hard about this market turned out to be wrong, and most of what I thought was easy turned out to be the actual product.
